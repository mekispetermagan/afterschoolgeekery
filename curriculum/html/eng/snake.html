Step 1: Drawing sprites

The tricky part of this game is designing the snake, since it changes its shape in action:

It is quite simply impossible to create a custome for every shape.

Instead, we create a sprite for the head, and another one for the segments of the snake's body; and we will create clones of the body segments to form the snake's body. It's simpler to show than to explain this.

Step 2: Moving the head

Once we can turn the snake, it's time to move it. In this type of game, the main sprite is moving continuously, the playser only has control over its direction. So you can create a separate script for the movement:

Step 3: Moving the body

The body of the snake is, so to say, growing from its head. The many segments are created continuously as the head moves. Since this part is probably new to the participants, and it is not easy to guess, feel free to show them that one sprite can in fact create a clone of the other one:

Now you can switch to the body sprite. The role of the body segments is simple: once they are created, they simply go to wherever the head is, and stay there.

And, tada!, the body starts following the snake, and the snake meandering around the stage.

But now the snake never stops growing. To ensure it has a fixed size, we segments should have a lifetime, being deleted after a few seconds. The higher the seconds, the longer the snake. Again, take your time playing around with numbers.

If you have reached this point, you can safely quit the project work with a feeling of success. You have created something spectacular; be proud of yourselves!

But if you are still ready to continue, many great steps are left to take in the project work; and presumably many of those will already be familiar to the kids.

Step 5: Make the snake eat and grow

The logic of the game is simple: when the snake eats, it grows. To implement it, first you need to introduce a new "food" sprite. you can draw it yourselves, or just use a ready-made sprite of Scratch, like an apple or a donut:

The food should appear at random points in the stage, waiting for being eaten. This sounds simple, but what does "being eaten" mean? Discuss this with the kids. You will conclude that the simplest way to implement this is that when food touches the snake's head, it disappears, and a bit later it appears again. This is now easy to code. We encourage you to use the "wait until" block here, because it is more intuitive and less error-prone than "if-then" in this context:

And how will the snake grow? We have already seen that the length of the snake depends on the lifetime of its segments; the longer the segments wait before being deleted, the longer the snake is. So, we need a variable to govern this. It may be a good idea to call it "length", but you can also call it "score", since it reflects the player's performance.
